Begin with the empty page. Not the content — the *empty page*. The first decision a layout makes is how much of itself it is willing to leave alone.

We call this whitespace, but the name is misleading. There is nothing *white* about it and nothing *empty*. Whitespace is the part of a design that holds its breath. It is the silence between notes, the margin around a painting, the pause before a sentence lands. And it is, more than any other element, the thing that separates a layout that feels *considered* from one that feels *crowded*.

## The fear of emptiness

Most layouts fail by addition. A client asks for "more presence." A stakeholder worries the page "looks empty." And so the whitespace fills, paragraph by paragraph, badge by badge, until the design is no longer a design but an inventory.

This fear is understandable. Emptiness reads as *risk*. A full page looks like it is working hard. An empty page looks like someone forgot something.

But the emptiness is the work. It is the space where the eye rests, where hierarchy becomes possible, where the things that *matter* get to matter. A page with no whitespace has no emphasis, because everything is shouting at the same volume.

> Whitespace is not what you didn't put on the page. It is what you chose not to.

## Three functions of space

Space in a layout does three jobs, and a good designer is conscious of all three.

**Separation.** Space tells the eye what belongs together and what belongs apart. Two paragraphs with a generous gap between them are two thoughts. Two paragraphs crushed together are one confused thought. The space *is* the punctuation.

**Emphasis.** The eye is drawn to isolation. A single element surrounded by space becomes, by virtue of that space, *important*. This is why a hero headline with nothing around it hits harder than the same headline buried in a grid — the emptiness is doing the emphasizing.

**Rhythm.** A page read top to bottom has a tempo. Consistent spacing creates a calm, even rhythm. Varied spacing — a tight cluster, then a breath, then another cluster — creates musicality. The reader feels this without naming it.

## The margin as a place

The word *margin* comes from the Latin *margo*, meaning border or edge. But in the medieval manuscript tradition, the margin was not a border. It was a *room* — a place where scribes wrote notes, where readers argued with the text, where tiny drawings of snails and rabbits staged absurd battles in the ink.

This is the spirit I try to keep. The margin is not wasted space. It is the space *reserved for the reader* — the place where their eye wanders, where their thought catches up with the text, where the design politely steps back.

A layout that fills its margins is a layout that has no room for the person reading it. And a design with no room for the reader is, in the end, just a display.
