There is a small structural feature at the end of every stroke in a serif typeface — a tiny flare, a foot, a finial — and for most of the last century a loud contingent of designers insisted it was obsolete. The sans-serif was the future. The serif was baggage. And yet here we are, still reading it.

The argument against serifs was always an argument about *modernity*, not about reading. Clean lines, the story went, suit a clean age. But the eye does not care about the age. The eye cares about the line, and the line is easier to follow when something marks its edges.

## What a serif actually does

A serif is a horizontal mark at the top and bottom of a vertical stroke. Its job is not ornamental. It does two things, both mechanical.

First, it extends the visual width of the letterform without extending its body, creating a subtle *baseline* and *cap line* that the eye uses to lock onto the row of text. This is why long passages set in a good serif feel *grounded* — there is a floor and a ceiling holding the letters in place.

Second, it creates a rhythmic texture of horizontal emphasis that pulls the gaze left to right, *along the line*, rather than encouraging it to drift up and down between lines. Sans-serifs, by contrast, emphasize the verticality of their strokes, which is beautiful for a single word on a sign and slightly less helpful for the seventh paragraph of an essay.

> A sans-serif is a wall. A serif is a shelf. You can lean on either, but only one holds the books.

## The readability question

The literature is a mess. Studies have been run since the 1920s comparing serif and sans-serif reading speeds, and the results are, charitably, inconclusive. The honest summary: **for fluent adult readers of long-form text, any well-designed typeface is readable**, and the differences measured in laboratories are smaller than the differences between a good and a bad afternoon.

But readability is not the whole story. There is also *settling* — the feeling, three paragraphs in, that you have stopped noticing the typeface and started living inside the words. This is where serifs quietly win. They recede because they were designed to recede. The little feet and brackets do their work and get out of the way.

## The case for character

The deeper argument for serifs is not about efficiency. It is about *character*.

A serif typeface carries five hundred years of revision in its bones. The proportions were argued over by punchcutters in Venice, by punchcutters in Garamond's workshop, by the engineers who translated metal into pixels. Each generation left a small inheritance in the curves. When you set a page in a serif, you are setting it in a *lineage*.

A great variable serif like **Fraunces** — the typeface you are reading now — makes this explicit. Its optical sizing axis means the letterforms *change* depending on how large they are: tighter and more robust at small sizes, more open and expressive at display sizes. The typeface is not one design. It is a *conversation* between sizes.

This is the quiet power. Not loud. Not modern in the 1920s sense. Just *considered*, in the way that a well-made chair is considered — the kind of object that does its job so well you forget someone designed it.

Until you sit in a bad one. And then you remember.
