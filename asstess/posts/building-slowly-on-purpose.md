There is a word that has crept into every design brief, every kickoff meeting, every retrospective in the last decade. The word is *velocity*. We measure it. We optimize for it. We build entire philosophies around it.

This essay is a small argument against it.

Not against speed itself — speed is sometimes the right answer. But against the assumption that speed is *always* the right answer, that faster is inherently better, that a design delivered in two weeks is twice as good as a design delivered in four. The math does not work. It has never worked.

## The cost of the first draft

The first design anyone makes for a problem is almost always the obvious one. It is the solution the brief *suggests*, the arrangement of parts that sits closest to the surface of the mind. Getting to it quickly is not hard. Getting *past* it is hard.

Velocity optimizes for the first draft. It rewards the team that ships the obvious solution first, declares the problem solved, and moves on. But the obvious solution is, by definition, the one everyone would arrive at — which means it is rarely the one that *fits*. It solves the problem as stated. It rarely solves the problem as *meant*.

> The first idea is the one you owe to the brief. The fifth idea is the one you owe to the work.

## What slowness buys

Slowness is not inefficiency. It is a *different economy* — one that trades calendar time for depth.

When you stay with a problem, three things happen that velocity forecloses.

**You notice the second problem.** Every brief states one problem and hides another. The fast team solves the stated one. The slow team has time to find the hidden one — the real constraint, the actual user, the thing that will break in month six.

**You find the non-obvious.** The third, fourth, and fifth ideas are where the interesting design lives. They require you to exhaust the obvious first, which takes time, and then to keep going, which takes discipline. Velocity rarely lets you keep going.

**You make fewer revisions.** A design shipped fast is often shipped *wrong*, and the cost of being wrong is paid later — in rework, in bugs, in the slow erosion of trust. Slowness front-loads the cost. It is cheaper, just not faster.

## The studio rhythm

In the studio, I think of this as the difference between *making* and *finishing*.

Making is fast. It is sketches, prototypes, rough drafts, the generation of options. You want a lot of it, and you want it loose.

Finishing is slow. It is the hundred small decisions — the kerning, the spacing, the exact weight of a rule, the moment a color shifts from *almost* to *right*. None of these decisions are dramatic. All of them are felt. And they cannot be rushed, because rushing them produces the thing every designer recognizes: work that is *done* but not *finished*.

## A modest proposal

You do not have to abandon speed. You have to *choose* it.

When the problem is well-understood and the solution is known, be fast. When the problem is fuzzy and the solution is not yet born, be slow. The skill is not in defaulting to either. The skill is in knowing which kind of problem is in front of you, and giving it the pace it deserves.

The best work I have made took longer than it should have. The best work I have admired, by others, almost always did too. There is no shame in this. There is only the quiet conviction that some things are worth the wait — and that the waiting is part of the work.
